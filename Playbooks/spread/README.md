# Spread 🏃‍♂️💨 (CFB26 Playbook Style)

**Author: Dave Voyles**

[← Back to Index](../README.md)

## Quick definition 🧠

The Spread is a **space-creating offense** that widens the defense horizontally so you can:

- ↔️ Run vs light boxes
- ⚡ Throw quick, high-efficiency passes
- 🧩 Use RPOs and tempo to keep the defense in bad looks

```text
      WR                      OL                       WR
               SB                               BS
        (Slot Receiver)                   (Big Slot/TE)
                          QB (Gun)
                             RB
```

Different teams “spread” differently. In games, the most common flavors are:

- **Spread-to-run** (zone read, QB run, RPO)
- **Spread-to-throw** (quick game, screens, option routes)
- **Balanced spread** (a bit of both)

## The identity (one sentence) 🎯

**Force the defense to defend every blade of grass, then take the easiest yards available on every snap.**

---

## Pros ✅ and cons ⚠️

### Pros

- **Easy box-count decisions**: the formation often tells you whether to run or throw.
- **Efficient QB reads**: many plays are one- or two-defender reads.
- **Tempo leverage**: you can prevent substitutions and keep vanilla looks.
- **Built-in answers**: bubbles, hitches, and quick outs punish soft coverage.
- **QB run threat** creates a numbers advantage that is hard to simulate with pure dropback.

### Cons

- If you overuse quick game, defenses will **jump routes**.
- Requires a QB who can **make decisions quickly** (and often run).
- Some spread books struggle in **short yardage** if you don’t carry a power package.
- Red zone can get crowded; you need **pick concepts** or **QB run** answers.

## Typical personnel (who’s on the field) 🧩

Spread playbooks commonly live in:

- **11 personnel**: 1 RB, 1 TE, 3 WR
  - The most flexible base (good for RPO, zone run, play-action).
- **10 personnel**: 1 RB, 0 TE, 4 WR
  - Maximum spacing; great for quick screens and box-count runs.
- **20 personnel**: 2 RB, 0 TE, 3 WR
  - “Pony” sets create mismatch headaches.

### Personnel cheat table

| Personnel | On-field           | Why you’d use it in Spread                        |
| --------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| 11        | 1 RB / 1 TE / 3 WR | Best all-around base for RPO + zone + play-action |
| 10        | 1 RB / 4 WR        | Maximum spacing; easiest box-count decisions      |
| 20        | 2 RB / 3 WR        | Pony mismatches; motion; option/RPO variants      |

### Role archetypes

- **QB:** quick processor, accurate short, plus mobility (even if not a true runner).
- **RB:** one-cut runner (zone), good in space, good hands.
- **Slot WR:** the engine—twitch + hands + RAC.
- **TE (if present):** conflict player on seams/sticks; can arc block on split flow.

## Core concept menu

### 1) Inside Zone (the foundation)

Even in a pass-happy spread, inside zone is your “keep them honest” run.

**When it’s great:**

- vs two-high shells with light boxes
- when defense is widening to stop screens

**Coaching point:** don’t force it. If A-gap is muddy, cut backside.

### 2) Outside Zone / Stretch

Use it to punish defenses that overload the middle.

### 3) Zone Read / Read Option (if your QB can run)

**What it does:** accounts for the backside defender by reading him.

**When to call:**

- vs aggressive ends who chase inside zone
- after you’ve shown inside zone a few times

**Key habit:** commit quickly—hesitation kills read plays.

### 4) Quick game: Stick, slants, hitches

Spread quick game is about **free access**.

Examples of “free access” cues:

- corner playing 7+ yards off: take hitch/now
- linebacker widened out of the box: run inside zone

### 5) Screens (bubble/now, tunnel, RB swing)

Screens are your perimeter run game.

- **Bubble/now**: punish off-coverage and soft flats.
- **Tunnel**: better vs corners that trigger on bubbles.
- **RB swing/flat**: great vs blitz and man.

### 6) RPOs (the spread cheat code)

RPOs are at their best when you treat them like a **decision framework**, not a random play call.

#### RPO rule #1: read the conflict defender

Common conflict defenders:

- apex defender (between slot and box)
- overhang safety
- inside linebacker to the passing side

If he fits the run, throw behind him.
If he widens to the pass, run.

#### RPO rule #2: keep your throws simple

Pick RPOs where the throw is:

- bubble/now
- quick slant/glance
- speed out

#### RPO rule #3: don’t get greedy

Take the 4–7 yards. The explosive comes later when the defense overcorrects.

### 7) Play-action glance / seams

Once quick game and RPOs widen the defense, you can hit:

- seam routes
- posts
- deep crossers

## Building your Spread call sheet (practical CFB26 plan)

### 1st and 10

- Inside zone
- Bubble/now (if cushion)
- Stick
- RPO bubble / RPO glance

### 2nd and short

- Shot play (post, seam)
- Read option
- Play-action glance

### 2nd and long

- Screens
- Mesh/shallow (if included)
- RPO that gives you a fast completion

### 3rd down

- 3rd & 1–2: inside zone, QB keep, quick out
- 3rd & 3–6: stick, spacing, slant/flat
- 3rd & 7+: dagger, verts, Y-cross (with a checkdown)

### Red zone (favorites)

- QB run threat (read option, QB power if available)
- Bunch/stack rubs
- Smash / corner routes
- RPOs with quick throws (but be careful; windows shrink)

### Two-minute

- Tempo + sidelines (outs, stop routes)
- Seam reads if defense stays two-high

## How defenses try to stop the Spread (and what to do)

### They play two-high and invite runs

- Run inside zone until they rotate a safety.
- Add RPO glance behind the LB if they overfit.

### They spin to single-high and crowd the box

- Hit quick perimeter throws (bubble/now).
- Call play-action seams/posts.

### They blitz and press

- RB swing, quick slants, tunnel screens.
- Use bunch/stack to get free releases.

### They “spy” the QB

- Make the spy wrong: throw quick game to replace him.
- If they remove a rusher to spy, you often have time for deeper concepts.

## Tempo strategy (how to use pace as a weapon)

Spread teams often talk about “playing fast,” but the best tempo is **selective**.

### When to go fast

- After a big gain (defense is disorganized)
- When the defense is stuck in a bad personnel grouping
- When you’ve found a concept they can’t align to cleanly (e.g., bubble looks free)

### When to slow down

- After a negative play (reset and don’t compound mistakes)
- In the red zone (decisions matter more than speed)
- When you’re protecting a lead (burn clock)

### A practical tempo rule

Run tempo until you get:

- a penalty
- a tackle for loss
- a 3rd down

Then slow down and call the next play with intent.

## RPO selection matrix (pick the right tag for the look)

If your playbook has a lot of RPOs, it can feel overwhelming. Use a simple “if-then” matrix.

### If the apex defender is tight to the box

- throw bubble/now
- throw quick out

### If linebackers are overfitting inside zone

- throw glance/slant behind them
- throw stick/spot behind the fit

### If corners are bailing and safeties are deep

- take hitches/now screens repeatedly
- run inside zone until they rotate down

### If they start jumping bubbles

- call a tunnel screen
- call a bubble-and-go / wheel if your book has it

## “Spread but not soft” (short yardage and goal-line plan)

One reason spread users struggle is they don’t carry a physical answer.

You don’t need a whole new offense—just a small package:

- inside zone from a tight split set
- QB power (if you have it)
- a bunch rub pass for 3rd & 2

If the defense goes dime to stop your spread looks, punish them with this package.

## Weekly install plan (build it in layers)

### Week 1: inside zone + bubble

- master box counts
- learn when bubble is “free”

### Week 2: add one RPO (glance)

- focus on reading the same conflict defender every time

### Week 3: add one QB run

- zone read or QB draw

### Week 4: add one shot play

- seams/verts or play-action post

The goal is to make your first 15 calls feel automatic.

## Troubleshooting

### Problem: “RPOs keep getting picked.”

Likely causes:

- throwing late (the window is quick)
- reading the wrong defender

Fix:

- pick one RPO and learn its key
- if you’re unsure, hand it off

### Problem: “I’m fast but I keep going 3-and-out.”

Likely cause:

- tempo without a plan (calling low-percentage plays repeatedly)

Fix:

- use a two-play pair: inside zone + quick throw
- only take shots on 2nd & short or after a big gain

### Problem: “QB run gets me injured.”

Fix:

- reduce designed QB runs in non-critical situations
- choose keepers where the QB can get down or out of bounds

## Box-count examples (turn formation into a decision)

One of the biggest Spread advantages is you can make the defense “declare” numbers.

### Example A: 2x2 gun set vs two-high

If both safeties are deep and corners are off, you often get a light box.

- If the box is light: inside zone.
- If the box is heavy: throw the free access (hitch/now).

### Example B: trips set with the defense sliding toward trips

Trips often pulls defenders toward the strong side.

- If the backside is isolated: take the 1-on-1 quick slant or hitch.
- If the defense overhang is tight: throw bubble/now to the trips side.

### Example C: defense walks a safety down late

That’s usually an attempt to steal your run numbers.

- If they rotate down: call play-action seam/post.
- If corners bail: quick game is still free.

The point isn’t to be perfect; it’s to stop calling runs into heavy boxes and passes into free-run looks.

## Concept pairing (how Spread stays unpredictable)

Pick a small set of “pairs” that look the same to the defense pre-snap.

Examples:

- Inside zone + RPO glance (same run action, different outcome)
- Bubble screen + tunnel screen (punish corners that trigger)
- Zone read + play-action seam (QB run threat creates hesitation)

If you always call bubble after inside zone, the defense will start jumping. Rotate pairs.

## Turnover avoidance (the spread killer)

Spread offenses lose games when they gift short fields.

Practical habits:

- Don’t throw late RPOs—if the read is unclear, hand it off.
- If you’re unsure on a shot play, check it down and live for 2nd & 6.
- When you’re ahead, reduce risk: run + quick game beats forcing explosives.

## Minimal glossary

- **RPO:** run-pass option; read one defender post-snap.
- **Conflict defender:** the defender who can’t cover both run and pass.
- **Free access:** uncovered or soft-covered receiver you can throw to instantly.

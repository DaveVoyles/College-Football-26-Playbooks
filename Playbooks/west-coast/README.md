# West Coast 🌊 (CFB26 Playbook Style)

Author: Dave Voyles

[← Back to Index](../README.md)

## Quick definition 🧠

The West Coast offense is a **timing + rhythm passing system** that treats:

- ⏱️ short and intermediate passes as the primary way to move the ball
- 🏃 run plays as complements that keep defenses honest
- 💨 **YAC (yards after catch)** as a core metric

```text
      WR                  OL   TE
                                     WR (Flanker)
             RB    QB    RB
              (Split Backs)
```

Compared to Air Raid, West Coast is often more:

- under-center / play-action oriented
- progression-based (1–2–3 reads)
- route-combo specific (concept names matter)

## The identity (one sentence) 🎯

**Win with on-time throws into defined windows, then let your skill players create YAC.**

---

## Pros ✅ and cons ⚠️

```diff
+ PRO: High completion percentage (rhythm/timing).
+ PRO: Elite vs Zone (layered concepts stress LBs).
+ PRO: Natural Play-Action (boots/rolls create easy reads).
+ PRO: YAC-Friendly (balls in space on the run).
+ PRO: Red Zone Versatility (quick outs/TE routes).

- CON: Requires perfect timing (late = INT).
- CON: Requires receivers who separate (contested catches are bad).
- CON: Vulnerable to Press Man (without stacks/bunch).
- CON: Underneath windows tighten if you abandon the run.
```

## Typical personnel 🧩

Common West Coast bases:

- **11 personnel**: 1 RB, 1 TE, 3 WR
  - Best all-around grouping.
- **12 personnel**: 1 RB, 2 TE, 2 WR
  - Adds formation diversity and stronger run looks.
- **21 personnel**: 2 RB, 1 TE, 2 WR
  - Classic pro feel; good for boots and play-action.

### Personnel cheat table

| **Personnel** | **On-field**       | **Why you’d use it in West Coast**                     |
| ------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| 11            | 1 RB / 1 TE / 3 WR | Best all-around rhythm menu + spacing                  |
| 12            | 1 RB / 2 TE / 2 WR | Stronger run look; more boots; TE-centric progressions |
| 21            | 2 RB / 1 TE / 2 WR | Classic play-action structure; strong protection looks |

### Role archetypes

- **QB:** accurate on short/intermediate, good anticipation.
- **RB:** reliable hands (checkdowns are real), good pass protection.
- **TE:** chain mover; must be able to sit in zones and win seams.
- **WRs:** route running + hands; at least one RAC threat on crossers.

## Core West Coast concept menu ⚙️

Below are “concept families” you’ll often find across West Coast playbooks.

### 1) Slant / Flat

**What it is:** a quick slant paired with a flat route.

**Why it works:** creates a simple high-low on the flat defender.

**Usage:**

- vs soft zone or off man
- in the red zone

**Coaching point:** throw the slant on time; don’t wait for it to look open.

### 2) Stick

Similar to Air Raid, but often used as a rhythm “get it out” call.

### 3) Drive (shallow cross + dig)

**What it is:** a shallow crosser underneath a deeper in-breaking route.

**Why it works:**

- vs man: traffic and leverage
- vs zone: stretches hook defenders

**QB rhythm:** shallow first if it’s clean; dig if LBs widen.

### 4) Levels

**What it is:** two routes at different depths breaking to the same side.

**Why it works:** forces one defender to cover two windows.

### 5) Sail / Flood (often off boot)

**What it is:** three-level stretch (flat + intermediate out + deep corner).

**Best when:** you can run the ball enough that boot looks believable.

### 6) Spot / Spacing variants

West Coast loves spacing concepts that create a stationary “spot” target in a zone void.

### 7) Smash

Corner + hitch. Great vs Cover 2 and in the red zone.

### 8) Screen / swing game

West Coast uses checkdowns intentionally:

- RB swing
- TE delay
- quick screens to punish blitz

## Building your West Coast call sheet (practical CFB26 plan) 🗂️

### Scripted opening drive (10–12 plays)

The point of a West Coast script is to **collect information**.

Example script goals:

- test if corners play press or off
- see if LBs widen with motion
- see if the defense rotates to single-high on early downs

Example play mix:

- inside zone
- slant/flat
- stick
- boot sail
- drive
- screen
- play-action shot (once)

### 1st and 10

- run (inside zone / duo / power)
- slant/flat or stick
- boot flood if your QB throws well on the move

### 2nd and short

- play-action corner/post
- deep cross

### 2nd and long

- drive / levels
- RB screen
- checkdown-friendly concepts

### 3rd down

- 3rd & 1–2: power, sneak, quick out
- 3rd & 3–6: slant/flat, stick, spacing
- 3rd & 7+: drive, dagger, flood with max protect

### Red zone (mini playbook)

- smash
- TE corner
- rub concepts from bunch/stack
- play-action leak

## Defensive answers and your counters 🛡️

### They play zone and rally

- Stay patient: chain together 5–8 yard completions.
- Use motion and flood/levels to create clearer windows.

### They press man

- Use bunch/stack formations.
- Call crossers/drive.
- Hit quick fades only if your matchup is elite.

### They blitz

- Hot reads: slants, quick outs, RB swing.
- Screens to slow them down.
- Consider max protect for your “big” 3rd-down concept.

## Timing and footwork cheat sheet (make the offense “feel” right) ⏱️

West Coast succeeds when you throw on time. A simple way to keep your rhythm consistent is to tie concepts to your QB’s drop.

### Quick game (instant / one-step)

Examples:

- slant/flat
- stick
- quick out
- hitch

**Coaching point:** decide pre-snap where the easy throw is. If the defense shows it, take it immediately.

### Rhythm game (short drop / normal timing)

Examples:

- drive (shallow first, dig second)
- levels
- spot/spacing

**Coaching point:** don’t wait for “perfect.” West Coast windows are often “NFL open”: throw when the receiver is about to enter the window.

### Shot game (play-action / longer timing)

Examples:

- deep cross
- post + over
- sail off boot with a deep corner

**Coaching point:** shot plays are called on purpose (2nd & short, after runs, after you’ve hit flats). If you call them randomly on 3rd & 11, you’ll feel like protection is “broken,” when it’s really sequencing.

## Progression rules (keep it simple) 🧭

Different playbooks label reads differently, but West Coast progressions usually follow a few repeatable patterns.

### Pattern 1: high-low the flat defender

Used in slant/flat, smash, and many boot floods.

```diff
+ GREEN: Flat Defender Widens -> THROW Inside (Slant/Hitch)
- RED:   Flat Defender Sits   -> THROW Outside (Flat/Out)
```

- If the flat defender widens: throw inside (slant/hitch).
- If the flat defender sits inside: throw the flat/out.

### Pattern 2: stretch the hook/curl defenders

Used in drive/levels/spot.

- If hook defenders widen with the shallow: hit the dig/spot behind them.
- If hook defenders sit: hit the shallow and run.

### Pattern 3: clear-out + intermediate window

Used in dagger-style concepts.

- The vertical route clears space.
- The dig/in route is the actual target.

**Tip:** if you struggle with “seeing” the dig window, call dagger toward the wide side of the field. You’ll get cleaner spacing.

## Motion and shifts (your best free information) 🔄

In CFB-style games, motion is the simplest way to identify coverage and steal leverage.

### Motion rules of thumb

- If a defender **travels** with the motion receiver: likely man.
- If the defense **bumps** zones and no one travels: likely zone.

### What to do with that info

- vs man: call drive/crossers or rubs from bunch/stack.
- vs zone: call spacing/spot/levels and be patient.

### “Easy leverage” motion uses

- Motion a WR into a reduced split to help him beat press.
- Motion a WR across to create a trips side and force zone adjustments.

## Protection and checkdowns (the invisible part of West Coast) 🛡️

West Coast doesn’t require you to bomb it deep every snap, but it does require you to avoid negative plays.

### How to think about protection

- If you’re calling a longer-developing concept, treat it like a **shot** and protect accordingly.
- If you’re calling quick game, don’t over-adjust protection; just get the ball out.

### Checkdown discipline

The checkdown isn’t a failure in West Coast—it’s part of the plan.

- If your first read is capped and your second is covered, take the RB/TE in the flat and live to play 2nd & 4.
- After two or three checkdowns, defenses start jumping—then your sail/corner routes open up.

## Troubleshooting (common reasons West Coast “doesn’t work”) 🧯

### Problem: “My routes are always covered.”

Likely causes:

- You’re waiting too long and letting zones settle.
- You’re calling the same concept into the same shell repeatedly.

Fix:

- Throw earlier.
- Alternate a spacing concept with a crosser concept.

### Problem: “I throw picks on slants.”

Likely causes:

- You’re throwing late vs lurking linebackers.

Fix:

- If you can’t throw it on time, don’t throw it. Take the flat or checkdown.

### Problem: “Boots get me sacked.”

Likely causes:

- You’re rolling into an unblocked edge.
- You’re drifting too deep.

Fix:

- Call boot away from the defense’s best edge rusher.
- Set the edge, then throw. Don’t keep backpedaling.

## YAC coaching points (how West Coast turns 6 into 18) 🏃

West Coast can feel “dinky” until you start designing catches that allow run-after-catch.

Practical ways to increase YAC:

- Favor **crossers** and **in-breaking routes** on early downs.
- Throw to the receiver’s upfield shoulder so he doesn’t have to stop.
- Use **motion** to create a free release and get the receiver moving at the snap.
- Call **play-action boots** when you expect defenders to flow hard to the run.

If you’re consistently completing passes but not breaking tackles, it often means you’re throwing stop routes all game. Mix in movement routes.

## In-game execution notes (small details that matter) 🎮

- Favor throws that keep receivers moving (crossers/drive) to maximize RAC.
- If you notice linebackers jumping underneath routes, call sail/flood or a deep cross to punish them.
- When you’re unsure, throw the checkdown; West Coast wins by stacking first downs.

## Post-Game Metrics (Did I run it right?) 📊

If you played a true West Coast game, your stats should look like this:

- **Completion Percentage:** > 75% (Short, high-percentage throws)
- **Yards Per Attempt:** ~7-8 (Efficiency over explosion)
- **Time of Possession:** > 55% (Ball control passing)
- **3rd Down Conversions:** > 50% (Manageable distances)

- **Sail/Flood:** three-level stretch to one sideline.

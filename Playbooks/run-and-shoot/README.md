# Run and Shoot (CFB26 Playbook Style)

## Quick definition

The Run and Shoot is a **spread passing offense** built around:

- four-receiver spacing (often 10 personnel)
- **option routes** where receivers adjust based on coverage/leverage
- attacking defenders who “miscommunicate”

Unlike many systems where routes are fixed, Run and Shoot asks:

- WRs to read coverage and convert routes
- QBs to throw to space, not to a pre-called spot

## The identity (one sentence)

**Stress the defense with four vertical threats and option routes until someone is wrong—then throw it there immediately.**

---

## Pros and cons

### Pros

- **Explosive passing potential**: big plays come from busted leverage.
- **Man/zone adaptable**: option routes can find grass vs zone and win leverage vs man.
- **Hard to “call” the defense into stops**: the offense can self-correct mid-play.
- **Great 2-minute offense**: spacing + sideline access.

### Cons

- Requires **chemistry**: QB and WR must read the same thing.
- Interception risk rises if you throw before confirming the receiver’s conversion.
- Protection can be an issue in empty/spread looks.
- Run game can be light unless your playbook includes strong draw/zone complements.

---

## Typical personnel

Classic Run and Shoot lives in:

- **10 personnel**: 1 RB, 0 TE, 4 WR

Common modern variants also use:

- **11 personnel**: 1 RB, 1 TE, 3 WR
  - Gives a seam/flat element and helps protection.

### Role archetypes

- **QB:** quick release, strong short/intermediate accuracy, calm under pressure.
- **WRs:** route running + awareness; at least one speed threat.
- **Slot WR(s):** option route specialists (change of direction + hands).
- **RB:** pass pro + checkdown skills; draw runner.

Run and Shoot is more about **alignment rules** than lots of formations.

Common families:

- Gun Spread / Wide
- Gun Trips
- Gun Empty
- Stack/slot variations (if the playbook has them)

Suggested “small menu”:

- Major: 2x2 spread + trips
- Minor: empty for 3rd-and-long and 2-minute

## Core concept menu (Run and Shoot staples)

Naming varies by playbook, but these ideas show up a lot.

### 1) Choice / Option routes (the centerpiece)

**What it is:** a receiver runs a route that converts based on leverage.

Common conversions:

- sit in zone vs soft coverage
- break out vs inside leverage
- break in vs outside leverage
- turn upfield vs “carry” by a flat defender

**QB coaching point:** don’t predetermine the break—confirm leverage and throw to space.

### 2) Seam reads / Four verts variants

Run and Shoot often attacks with vertical stems and seam decisions.

**When to call:**

- after defenses squat on option routes
- vs single-high when you can isolate an outside receiver

### 3) Switch releases

**What it is:** receivers cross at the line (often slot and outside) to create rubs and confuse match coverage.

**Why it works:** forces communication; beats some pattern-match rules.

### 4) Shallow / crossers (the “get out of jail”)

Even a vertical offense needs a fast answer vs pressure.

- shallow cross
- quick outs
- RB swing

### 5) Draw / inside zone

Run and Shoot run game is often:

- draw
- inside zone (from gun)

The goal is not 40 carries; it’s to punish light boxes and keep pass rush honest.

---

## The Run and Shoot decision model (simple and usable)

### Step 1: identify the shell

- 1-high: expect more man and single-high zones; vertical shots become attractive.
- 2-high: expect soft zones/match rules; option routes and seams become attractive.

### Step 2: locate the “conflict” defender

Often it’s an apex/overhang who must cover:

- the slot option route
- the flat/screen
- the run fit

Make him wrong.

### Step 3: throw to the first clear win

Don’t hunt the perfect play. Take the first clean window to stay ahead of the sticks.

## Building your Run and Shoot call sheet (practical CFB26 plan)

### 1st and 10

- choice/option concept
- quick hitch/now screen (if cushion)
- inside zone / draw (if light box)

### 2nd and short

- four verts / seams
- switch vertical concept

### 2nd and long

- shallow cross / quick outs
- RB screen
- choice concept with built-in checkdown

### 3rd down

- 3rd & 1–2: inside zone, quick out
- 3rd & 3–6: option routes (slots), spacing
- 3rd & 7+: seams/verts with max protect, or a “levels” concept if available

### Red zone (packages)

Option routes still work but windows tighten.

- bunch/stack rubs (if in your book)
- smash (corner/hitch)
- quick slants with leverage

## Defensive answers and your counters

### They play press man

- Use stack/bunch alignments.
- Call switch releases and quick crossers.
- If your WR wins, take the fade; if not, don’t force it.

### They sit in two-high and rally

- Keep taking option routes underneath.
- Use draws/inside zone to punish light boxes.
- Mix in seam shots when safeties get nosy.

### They blitz

- Hot answers: shallow, RB swing, quick outs.
- Screens to slow down pressure.
- Consider keeping RB in and throwing a quick seam/option.

## Option routes in practice (how to stop throwing “wrong-route” picks)

Run and Shoot lives or dies on QB/WR agreement. In-game, you don’t have full real-life route language, so you need simple, repeatable rules.

### The three leverage questions

Before you throw an option route, answer:

1. **Is it man or zone?** (use motion to check)
2. **Is the defender inside or outside leverage?**
3. **Is there safety help over the top?**

You don’t need perfection—just enough information to avoid the worst throws.

### Practical option-route outcomes (common conversions)

You’ll see these most often:

- **Sit** vs soft zone: receiver throttles in grass.
- **Break out** vs inside leverage: receiver protects the throw.
- **Break in** vs outside leverage: receiver uses the defender’s position against him.
- **Turn upfield** vs squat/flat defenders: receiver converts to a seam.

**QB rule of thumb:** if you can’t tell which way the receiver will break, throw the checkdown. The Run and Shoot is allowed to take 5 yards.

## How to call Run and Shoot (sequencing matters)

This style is most explosive when you alternate:

- option routes (to force defenders to hesitate)
- verticals/switch (to punish hesitation)
- draw/inside zone (to punish two-high/light boxes)

### A simple 6-play sequence that works

1. Choice/option (get a completion)
2. Draw/inside zone (punish light box)
3. Choice/option to the other side (make them cover the whole field)
4. Switch vertical (shot)
5. Shallow/quick out (blitz answer)
6. Four verts or seams (second shot)

The point is to keep the defense from guessing “option routes all drive.”

## Protection plan (how to survive in spread sets)

Many Run and Shoot looks are pass-first and can invite pressure.

### Your protection toolbox

- **Quick game** as protection: if you throw it fast, pressure doesn’t matter.
- **RB in protection**: especially on 3rd-and-long.
- **Max protect** for your favorite shot concept.

### When to take the sack vs force the ball

If your first two options are covered and the checkdown is guarded, a sack is sometimes better than a pick-six. Run and Shoot can win with volume—turnovers are the real killer.

Goal: learn box counts and throw timing.

### Week 2: add one shot

- switch vertical or four verts variant

### Week 4: add one red zone plan

- smash + rub concept

Goal: touchdowns, not field goals.

## Troubleshooting

### Problem: “My receiver sits but I threw the out (pick).”

Fix:

- use motion more to identify man/zone
- be willing to throw the checkdown instead of guessing the conversion

### Problem: “I’m getting sacked in empty.”

Fix:

- don’t live in empty on early downs
- keep RB in on your shot plays
- call a screen/quick out when you feel pressure coming

### Problem: “I complete passes but never get explosives.”

Fix:

- call switch vertical after you’ve hit two option completions
- call seams when safeties start squatting

## Coverage examples (what to look for in real time)

This section is intentionally “pattern based,” since in-game labels can be imperfect.

### Versus two-high zone shells

What you’ll usually get:

- space underneath if the defense is patient
- safeties sitting on verticals

What to do:

- take option routes that settle in the hook/curl voids
- call draw/inside zone to punish light boxes
- take seam shots only when safeties start triggering downhill

### Versus single-high zone shells

What you’ll usually get:

- a middle safety who can erase one vertical
- outside corners protecting deep thirds

What to do:

- attack the seams if you can hold the middle safety
- call flood/levels variants (if in your book) to overload a side
- take the checkdown if the middle is capped

### Versus man coverage

What you’ll usually get:

- defenders playing leverage and trying to undercut option breaks

What to do:

- use motion to create stacks and free releases
- call switch concepts (natural rubs)
- throw away from leverage (outside vs inside leverage, inside vs outside leverage)

## QB/WR chemistry drills (practice-mode routine)

Because option routes convert, your best “training” is repetition with intent.

Suggested routine (10–15 minutes):

1. Call your main choice/option concept vs a zone look. Repeat until you can consistently identify the settle window.
2. Call the same concept vs man. Focus on leverage: inside vs outside.
3. Add a switch vertical shot after two completions to simulate game sequencing.
4. Finish with your blitz answer (shallow/swing) so your brain doesn’t panic when pressure shows up.

Goal: you should be able to say “I’m throwing the slot option unless the defender’s leverage says otherwise” and execute without hesitation.

## Minimal glossary

- **Option route:** receiver adjusts route based on leverage/coverage.
- **Switch release:** two receivers exchange lanes/releases to create rub/confusion.
- **Match coverage:** zone-man hybrid where defenders carry routes based on rules.
